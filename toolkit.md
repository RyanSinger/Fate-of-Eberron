The GM's Guide tells you the rules and the setting. This document tells you what to actually do with them. It's a companion, not a replacement. If the GM's Guide is the map, this is the driving directions: when to use each system, how to structure your sessions, and what to do between them to keep the campaign feeling alive.

## Section 1: The GM Workflow

### The Loop

Everything in this toolkit fits into a repeating cycle. Here's the full picture:

| Phase | What Happens | Toolkit System |
|-------|-------------|----------------|
| The Pitch | Propose a game concept. Gauge interest. | None yet. |
| Session Zero | Build Story Elements, Charter, Region. Create characters. | Story Elements, Charter (players guide), Region System |
| Post Session Zero | Reconcile player input with your ideas. Create NPCs from character aspects and charter antagonists. Sketch the first arc. | Arc Generation (below) |
| Arc Generation | Who wants what? Who opposes them? Why do PCs care? What happens if nobody intervenes? | Questions and Decisions |
| Session Prep | What changed? What are NPCs and factions doing? What hand grenades can you lob? Look at PC aspects for compels. | Downtime (GM actions), Compels |
| Running the Session | Start of session check in. Frame scenes. Resolve scenes. Lob hand grenades. End with downtime. | Downtime, Compels, Region System |
| Ending the Session | Milestones. Downtime actions. Feedback. | Downtime, Advancement (players guide) |
| Between Sessions | Update region map. Advance faction agendas. Track countdowns. Prep next session. | Region System, Downtime (GM actions) |

You don't move through these phases once. You loop. Arc Generation feeds into Session Prep feeds into Running the Session feeds into Between Sessions and back into Arc Generation.

---

### Arc Generation

An arc is a situation with stakes. It's not a plot. Plots have predetermined outcomes. Situations don't.

Before you start an arc, answer five questions:

1. **Why is this relevant to the characters?** Tie it to character aspects. If you can't connect the arc to at least one PC's aspects, find a different arc. Or change the arc until it fits.
2. **Who is involved?** Name the NPCs and factions. Give them faces.
3. **What are they trying to achieve?** Define their goal clearly. Vague goals make for vague drama.
4. **Who opposes them?** Not just the PCs. Other factions, rival NPCs, competing interests. The world has friction.
5. **What happens if nobody intervenes?** This is your countdown. Write it down. It gives the situation urgency without forcing the players to act.

The best arcs come from character creation. In Fate, player backstories aren't backstories. They're the story. When players write their five aspects and contribute to the charter, they're handing you NPCs, factions, threats, and relationships. The antagonists listed in the charter are the antagonists of your first arc. The faces in a character's aspects are your supporting cast. You don't have to invent much. You just have to pay attention.

---

### Session Prep

Good prep is about knowing the situation, not predicting what happens. Here are five steps:

1. **What changed?** How did last session's events affect the world? Did a faction gain ground? Did an NPC get what they wanted, or get stopped? Update the region map if needed.
2. **What are the NPCs doing?** Every active NPC has a goal. What's their next move, given what happened last session? They don't wait for the PCs to show up.
3. **Look at character sheets.** Which aspects are ripe for compels? Which consequences are still in play? Consequences are story hooks that players already bought into.
4. **Prep hand grenades.** A hand grenade is an event that demands a PC response without dictating what that response is. An NPC shows up asking for help. A secret gets revealed. A faction makes a move. Something blows up, literally or figuratively. Tie hand grenades to PC aspects whenever possible. They land harder.
5. **Don't predict.** The more you predict what will happen, the more you'll try to make it happen. Go in knowing the situation. Don't know the outcome.

---

### Running the Session

Six steps, in order:

1. **Start of session check in.** Ask players what their characters have been up to. This grounds everyone and sometimes generates material you can use immediately.
2. **Frame scenes.** Drive toward interesting decisions. Before you open a scene, ask yourself: what is this scene about? What's at stake? What could go wrong? If you can't answer those questions, you're probably not in the right scene yet.
3. **Ask "What do you do?"** Let the players drive. If they stall or look at you waiting for something to happen, lob a hand grenade. Don't narrate a path. Create pressure.
4. **Resolve scenes.** Use the right pacing tool: a simple roll, a challenge, a contest, or a conflict. After resolution, tell the players what changed and let them react. The reaction is often more interesting than the resolution.
5. **End with downtime.** Each PC takes a downtime action. You take your GM downtime actions. This is where faction agendas advance and countdowns tick forward.
6. **Solicit feedback.** What worked? What didn't? What do players want more of? You don't have to act on every answer, but you need to hear them.

---

### The Most Important Rule

Don't plan what will happen.

Plan the situation: who wants what, who's in the way, what's at stake, and what happens if nobody does anything. Then sit down at the table and find out what the characters do about it.

That's the job.

## Eberron Story Elements

During Session Zero, after choosing a Charter framework but before building the region or individual characters, the group fills out a Story Elements worksheet. Three steps.

### Step 1: Inspirations

The group brainstorms media, tropes, and themes that excite them. No bad suggestions. Write them all down.

> *Blade Runner, Legend of Korra, Leverage, political intrigue, ancient mysteries, found family*

### Step 2: Story Ingredients

Go through the Eberron ingredient categories and sort each into Keepers (we want this), Deal Breakers (avoid this), or Conditional (fine under certain conditions, specify what).

| Category | Ingredients to Discuss |
|----------|----------------------|
| **Tone** | Pulp adventure, noir mystery, horror, political drama, heist/caper, comedy, romance, tragedy |
| **Scope** | Neighborhood, city, national, continental, planar |
| **The Last War** | Background texture, active wound, resuming conflict, veteran trauma, war crimes, refugees |
| **Factions** | Which Dragonmarked Houses are active? Which criminal organizations? Which governments? Which secret powers? |
| **The Draconic Prophecy** | Not present, background mystery, central plot driver |
| **The Planes** | Manifest zones as color, planar threats, planar travel, specific planes (Thelanis, Dal Quor, Xoriat, Shavarath) |
| **Horror** | Daelkyr and aberrations, Vol and undead, the Mournland, Khyber, body horror, psychological horror |
| **Identity and Prejudice** | Warforged personhood, changeling distrust, shifter discrimination, goblinoid marginalization |
| **Magic and Technology** | Artifice and invention, dragonshards, creation forges, eldritch machines |

- **Keepers**: Elements the group wants. No one objects.
- **Deal Breakers**: Elements to avoid entirely. If one player flags it, it's off the table for everyone.
- **Conditional**: Allowed under specific conditions the group agrees on. Examples: "Horror is fine as long as it's cosmic/existential, not body horror" or "The Last War can be background for NPCs but we don't want flashback scenes."

This is an ideal time to introduce or review safety tools (X-Card, Lines and Veils).

### Step 3: Campaign Aspects

Distill the keepers and inspirations into 2-4 world aspects that apply to every session. These are always true, always invokable, always compellable.

> *The Treaty of Thronehold Is Held Together by Lies, Nobody Trusts a Changeling, The Mourning Could Happen Again, Sharn Runs on Favors and Debts*

### Between Sessions

Revisit the Story Elements worksheet before each session:

- **Review the elements.** Are the keepers, deal breakers, and conditionals still working? Anything to change just for this session?
- **Summarize themes.** Give the players an overview of the themes planned for this session. Do they have feedback or concerns?
- **Monitor the action.** During the game, watch for signs of discomfort and check in when things get intense.
